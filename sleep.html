Sleep During Menopause — A Natural Protocol

← mirishi.com

Poor sleep is one of the most common and disruptive symptoms of perimenopause and menopause. This page gives you a complete natural protocol — acupressure points, medicinal herbs, and essential oils — with exact locations, dosages, and timing. No supplements to guess at. No vague advice.

Why sleep changes during menopause

Declining oestrogen disrupts the brain's temperature regulation (the hypothalamus) and reduces serotonin — the precursor to melatonin. The result: you can't fall asleep, you wake at 3am, or you wake drenched in sweat.

From a Traditional Chinese Medicine perspective, oestrogen decline corresponds to a weakening of Kidney Yin — the cooling, nourishing, grounding force in the body. When Yin is deficient, Yang heat rises at night, the Heart Shen (spirit/mind) becomes unsettled, and the mind cannot quieten enough for deep sleep.

Both frameworks point to the same experience. The natural protocol below addresses both the physiological and energetic causes.

4 Acupressure Points for Sleep

Press each point firmly with your thumb or middle finger. Hold for 2–3 minutes per side, breathing slowly. Best done lying in bed before sleep.

Point 1 — Primary

HT7 — Spirit Gate (神門)

Location: On the wrist crease, little-finger side, in the hollow beside the pisiform bone

The most important point for insomnia and anxiety. Calms the Heart Shen — the mind held in the Heart in TCM. When HT7 is stimulated, mental chatter quietens and the nervous system downregulates. Press 2–3 minutes on each wrist.

Point 2

PC6 — Inner Gate (內關)

Location: Inner forearm, 3 finger-widths above the wrist crease, between the two central tendons

Calms the Heart and opens the chest. Reduces palpitations that often accompany menopausal sleep disruption. Combine with HT7 for stronger effect — press both points for 2 minutes each side.

Point 3

SP6 — Three Yin Meeting (三陰交)

Location: Inside of the leg, 4 finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the tibia

The intersection of three Yin meridians — Kidney, Liver, Spleen. Nourishes Yin directly, calms the mind, and regulates the hormonal axis. 2 minutes each side. Avoid during pregnancy.

Point 4

KD6 — Shining Sea (照海)

Location: 1 cun (thumb-width) directly below the centre of the inner ankle bone

Opens the Yin Qiao vessel — the channel that regulates the sleep-wake cycle. KD6 is used specifically for insomnia where the mind is active at night and the body cannot rest. 1–2 minutes each side.

3 Medicinal Herbs for Sleep

Herb 1 — Most studied

Valerian (Valeriana officinalis)

Dose: 300–600mg standardised extract, 30–45 minutes before bed.
How it works: Increases GABA availability in the brain — the same mechanism as benzodiazepines, without the dependency or sedation hangover. Reduces time to sleep onset. Allow 2 weeks of consistent use before judging effectiveness.
Note: A small percentage of people find valerian stimulating rather than sedating — if that's you, try passionflower instead.

Herb 2

Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)

Dose: Tea: 2 teaspoons dried herb, steep 10 minutes, 1 hour before bed. Tincture: 40 drops in water, three times daily.
How it works: Binds to GABA receptors and reduces repetitive anxious thoughts — the kind that keep the mind circling at 3am. Particularly useful when sleep disruption is accompanied by anxiety or mild depression.
Note: Safe for long-term use. Gentle enough to take during the day for background anxiety.

Herb 3 — Adaptogen

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) — KSM-66 extract

Dose: 300mg KSM-66 extract, taken with dinner.
How it works: Lowers cortisol levels, particularly evening cortisol — elevated cortisol is one of the primary reasons for waking between 2–4am during perimenopause. Clinical trials show 300mg KSM-66 taken for 8 weeks reduces cortisol by 27% and significantly improves sleep quality scores.
Note: This is an 8-week minimum protocol. Results build over time.

3 Essential Oils for Sleep

Oil 1 — Most evidence

Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)

How to use: 2 drops on your pillow, or 4 drops in a diffuser running for 30 minutes before bed (switch off when you sleep).
Why it works: Linalool and linalyl acetate modulate GABA receptors and reduce sympathetic nervous system activity. Multiple clinical trials confirm improved sleep quality and reduced night waking with lavender inhalation.

Oil 2 — Grounding

Vetiver (Vetiveria zizanioides)

How to use: 1 drop on the soles of the feet before sleep. The scent is earthy, heavy, and grounding — it anchors the mind when thoughts are racing.
Why it works: The deepest-rooted plant used in aromatherapy. Corresponds in TCM to Earth energy — settling, centring, calming excessive mental activity. Particularly useful for the type of insomnia where the mind will not stop replaying the day.

Oil 3 — Nervous system

Roman Chamomile (Anthemis nobilis)

How to use: 2 drops in 1 teaspoon of jojoba oil, massage into temples and inner wrists before bed.
Why it works: Apigenin content acts on the same receptors as chamomile tea but more concentrated. Gentle sedative and antispasmodic — relaxes physical tension held in the body, which then allows the mind to follow.

The evening protocol — how to combine everything

  1. 7–8pm: Take ashwagandha (300mg) with dinner
  2. 9pm: Drink passionflower tea while it steeps, or take valerian capsule
  3. 9:30pm: Diffuse lavender for 30 minutes while winding down
  4. Before bed: 1 drop vetiver on soles of feet
  5. In bed: Work through the 4 acupressure points (HT7, PC6, SP6, KD6) — 2 minutes each side, breathing slowly

The full sequence takes about 10 minutes in bed. Most people notice improvement within the first week. The deeper effect of ashwagandha builds over 6–8 weeks.

What not to combine

  • Do not combine valerian with alcohol, antihistamines, or sedative medications
  • Avoid ashwagandha if you have thyroid conditions without consulting a doctor
  • SP6 acupressure point: avoid during pregnancy
  • Do not diffuse essential oils continuously through the night — 30 minutes maximum
