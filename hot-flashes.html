Hot flashes affect up to 80% of women during menopause. This page gives you a complete natural protocol — what to press, what to take, and what to inhale — with exact dosages and timing. Based on Traditional Chinese Medicine, clinical herbal research, and aromatherapy evidence.

What causes hot flashes

Hot flashes are caused by declining oestrogen destabilising the hypothalamus — the brain's thermostat. The hypothalamus interprets normal body temperature as overheating and triggers a cooling response: blood vessels dilate, sweat glands activate, and you feel the wave of heat rising from chest to face.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, this is understood as Kidney Yin deficiency. Yin is the cooling, moistening, grounding principle. When it weakens with age, Yang heat rises unchecked — especially at night when Yin should be dominant. The treatment principle is to nourish Yin and anchor the rising heat downward.

Both approaches inform the protocol below.

4 Acupressure Points for Hot Flashes

Use firm, steady pressure — not rubbing. Hold each point for 2 minutes per side. Best done twice daily: morning and before bed. During a flash, press KD1 immediately.

Point 1 — Emergency point

KD1 — Bubbling Spring (湧泉)

Location: On the sole of the foot, in the deep depression at the junction of the front third and rear two-thirds — when the toes are flexed, it is the deepest point

The most powerful point for drawing heat downward. In TCM, heat rises to the head and face; KD1 is the lowest point of the body — pressing it grounds the rising Yang and anchors heat back to its source. Press both feet firmly for 2 minutes during or after a flash. You can also rub vigorously to generate warmth and circulation downward.

Point 2 — Yin nourishing

KD7 — Recovering Flow (復溜)

Location: 2 cun (3 finger-widths) directly above KD3, on the inside of the ankle, anterior to the Achilles tendon

Tonifies Kidney Yin and regulates sweating — used specifically for night sweats in classical TCM. KD7 works more slowly than KD1 but addresses the root cause rather than the symptom. Include in daily morning practice. 2 minutes each side.

Point 3 — Three Yin

SP6 — Three Yin Meeting (三陰交)

Location: 4 finger-widths above the inner ankle bone, just behind the posterior border of the tibia

Intersection of the Kidney, Liver, and Spleen meridians — nourishes all three Yin systems simultaneously. Essential for any hormonal protocol. 2 minutes each side. Avoid during pregnancy.

Point 4 — Heart calming

PC6 — Inner Gate (內關)

Location: Inner forearm, 3 finger-widths above the wrist crease, between the two central tendons

Calms the Heart and reduces palpitations — the racing heart that often accompanies a hot flash. Also useful for the anxiety that builds in anticipation of flashes in social situations. 1–2 minutes each side.

3 Herbs for Hot Flashes

Herb 1 — Most evidence

Sage (Salvia officinalis)

Dose: Tea: 1 teaspoon dried sage leaf, steep 5 minutes, 2 cups daily. Or tincture: 3ml three times daily.
Why it works: Sage acts on the same thermoregulatory pathways as the hypothalamus, reducing the frequency and intensity of sweating episodes. Clinical trials show significant reduction in hot flash frequency within 4 weeks. Also has mild phytoestrogenic activity.
Note: Avoid in epilepsy. Do not use during pregnancy. Sage essential oil is different from culinary/herbal sage — the herb is safe; the essential oil requires caution.

Herb 2 — Phytoestrogenic

Black Cohosh (Actaea racemosa)

Dose: Standardised extract, 20mg at 2.5% triterpene glycosides, twice daily. Take consistently for 3 months minimum.
Why it works: Black cohosh binds to oestrogen receptors in the hypothalamus specifically — the area responsible for thermoregulation. It does not raise systemic oestrogen levels (it is not estrogenic in breast tissue) but acts selectively where hot flashes originate. This is why it is generally considered safe for women with oestrogen-sensitive conditions — but always confirm with your doctor.
Note: Must be standardised extract for reliable dosing. Loose herb teas are not effective at therapeutic levels.

Herb 3 — Adaptogen

Maca (Lepidium meyenii)

Dose: 1–2 teaspoons powder in morning smoothie or 500–1000mg capsule with breakfast. Gelatinised form is easier to digest.
Why it works: Maca works on the HPA axis — the hormone feedback loop between hypothalamus, pituitary, and adrenal glands. Rather than replacing hormones, it improves the body's own hormone regulation. Peruvian clinical studies show reduction in hot flash frequency and intensity, plus improved mood and energy. Effect builds over 6–8 weeks.
Note: Not suitable for oestrogen-receptor-positive breast cancer without medical guidance.

3 Essential Oils for Hot Flashes

Oil 1 — Immediate cooling

Peppermint (Mentha piperita)

How to use: 1 drop on the back of the neck during a flash. Keep away from eyes and mucous membranes. Do not apply undiluted to large skin areas.
Why it works: Menthol activates TRPM8 receptors — the body's cold-sensing receptors — creating an immediate sensation of cooling. It does not lower actual body temperature but dramatically reduces the subjective experience of heat. Carry a small roller bottle (1 drop peppermint in 5ml jojoba) for use anywhere.

Oil 2 — Hormonal

Clary Sage (Salvia sclarea)

How to use: 2 drops on inner wrists morning and evening, diluted in a few drops of jojoba or almond oil. Or 3 drops in an evening bath.
Why it works: Contains sclareol — a phytoestrogen precursor that interacts with oestrogen receptors. Regular use over 4–6 weeks can reduce flash frequency. Distinct from culinary sage — clary sage is specifically used for hormonal support and has a warm, earthy scent.
Note: Do not confuse with sage essential oil (Salvia officinalis) — they are different plants with different safety profiles.

Oil 3 — Balancing

Geranium (Pelargonium graveolens)

How to use: 2 drops behind the ears daily, or 4 drops diffused in the evening.
Why it works: Geranium is considered a hormonal regulator in clinical aromatherapy — it supports the adrenal cortex and helps balance cortisol alongside oestrogen. Works well combined with clary sage. The scent is floral and balancing, making it suitable for daytime use in work environments.

Daily protocol — how to combine everything

  1. Morning: Maca powder in smoothie + 2 drops clary sage on wrists (diluted)
  2. Morning practice (5–10 min): KD1, KD7, SP6 acupressure — 2 minutes each side
  3. Midday: Cup of sage tea
  4. Afternoon: Second black cohosh dose
  5. During a flash: 1 drop peppermint on back of neck + press KD1 immediately
  6. Evening: Geranium diffused + PC6 and SP6 acupressure before bed

What to expect and when

  • Peppermint oil: Immediate effect during a flash
  • Sage herb: Noticeable reduction in sweating within 2–4 weeks
  • Black cohosh: 4–8 weeks for full effect; do not judge before 3 months
  • Maca: 6–8 weeks for hormonal effect; energy improvement often noticed sooner
  • Acupressure: KD1 works immediately; regular practice reduces baseline frequency over 4–6 weeks
