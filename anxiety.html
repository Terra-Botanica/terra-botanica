Anxiety during hormonal transitions — perimenopause, menopause, postpartum — is among the most common and least talked-about symptoms. This page gives you a complete natural protocol using acupressure, medicinal herbs, and essential oils, with exact dosages and a daily practice you can start today.

Why anxiety increases during hormonal change

Oestrogen has a direct regulatory effect on the nervous system — it modulates GABA receptors (the brain's main inhibitory system) and supports serotonin production. As oestrogen fluctuates and declines, GABA tone drops, and the nervous system becomes more reactive. Small stressors feel large. The sense of dread arrives without obvious cause. Sleep disruption worsens everything.

Cortisol — the stress hormone — also tends to rise during perimenopause, particularly evening cortisol. This creates a physiological state of chronic low-level threat, which the mind then looks for reasons to justify.

In Traditional Chinese Medicine, anxiety maps onto two patterns. The first is Liver Qi stagnation — emotion, frustration, and stress that cannot move through the body become trapped, creating internal pressure, irritability, and chest tightness. The second is Heart Shen disturbance — the mind (Shen) held in the Heart cannot settle, creating racing thoughts, restlessness, and a disconnected feeling. Both patterns are addressed by the protocol below.

4 Acupressure Points for Anxiety

Slow, sustained pressure is most effective — not rubbing or tapping. Hold each point for 2–3 minutes per side, breathing slowly into the belly. You can do all four points in 15 minutes.

Point 1 — Primary

PC6 — Inner Gate (內關)

Location: Inner forearm, 3 finger-widths above the wrist crease, between the two tendons in the centre

The most clinically documented point for anxiety, nausea, and chest tightness. Opens the chest, calms the Heart, regulates the Shen. When the chest feels constricted and you cannot take a full breath, PC6 directly addresses this. Press 2–3 minutes each side. This is also the acupressure point on which most sea-sickness wristbands are based — its calming action is well established.

Point 2

HT7 — Spirit Gate (神門)

Location: Wrist crease, on the little-finger side, in the hollow just beside the pisiform bone

Directly calms the Heart Shen — the mental-emotional centre of TCM. HT7 addresses overthinking, fear, excessive worry, and the anxious mind that cannot rest. It works well combined with PC6 — press both together for 2 minutes each wrist. Particularly useful in the evening before bed or at the onset of a panic or anxiety episode.

Point 3 — Liver Qi

LR3 — Great Surge (太衝)

Location: On the top of the foot, in the depression between the 1st and 2nd metatarsal bones, 2 cun back from the web margin between the toes

The primary point for moving stagnant Liver Qi — the trapped emotional energy that creates irritability, frustration, and the physical sensation of pressure building. LR3 releases what cannot express itself. It is often tender or sensitive on anxious people — that sensitivity confirms it needs attention. Press 2 minutes each foot. You can press more firmly here than at other points.

Point 4 — Mind calming

GV20 — Hundred Convergences (百會)

Location: Top of the head, at the midpoint of a line connecting the two ear apices — the highest point of the skull

All Yang channels converge here. GV20 calms excessive mental activity, lifts depression, and clears the head. Use light circular massage rather than firm pressure — 1–2 minutes. Particularly useful when anxiety manifests as mental fog, dizziness, or a disconnected feeling. Also helps with the sense of pressure or heaviness in the head that accompanies chronic stress.

3 Herbs for Anxiety

Herb 1 — Adaptogen

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) — KSM-66 extract

Dose: 300mg KSM-66 extract twice daily, with food. Morning and evening doses.
Why it works: The most clinically studied adaptogen for anxiety and cortisol reduction. Multiple randomised controlled trials show KSM-66 reduces serum cortisol by 27%, reduces anxiety scores significantly, and improves stress resilience. The effect is physiological — it directly modulates the HPA axis (the cortisol production system), not just the subjective experience. 8-week minimum protocol.
Note: Avoid in thyroid conditions without medical guidance. Safe for long-term use at standard doses.

Herb 2 — GABA support

Passionflower (Passiflora incarnata)

Dose: Tea: 2 teaspoons dried herb, steep 10 minutes, 2–3 cups daily. Tincture: 40 drops in water, three times daily.
Why it works: Passionflower's active compounds bind to GABA-A receptors — the same receptors targeted by benzodiazepines — producing a calming effect without sedation or dependency. Particularly effective for anxiety characterised by repetitive, looping thoughts. Safe for long-term daily use. One clinical trial found passionflower equivalent to oxazepam (a benzodiazepine) for generalised anxiety, with fewer side effects.
Note: Can enhance the effect of sedative medications. Allow 1–2 weeks for full effect.

Herb 3 — Cooling and calming

Lemon Balm (Melissa officinalis)

Dose: Tea: 2 teaspoons fresh or 1 teaspoon dried, steep 5 minutes, 2–3 cups daily. Tincture: 2ml three times daily.
Why it works: Inhibits GABA transaminase — the enzyme that breaks down GABA — allowing GABA to remain active longer in the nervous system. Also has direct anxiolytic and mild antidepressant effects via monoamine oxidase inhibition. One of the safest medicinal herbs available, with thousands of years of traditional use. Works well combined with passionflower — the combination is more effective than either alone.
Note: May mildly reduce thyroid function — avoid in hypothyroidism. Otherwise extremely safe.

3 Essential Oils for Anxiety

Oil 1 — Most evidence

Lavender (Lavandula angustifolia)

How to use: 2 drops on inner wrists and inhale, or diffuse 4 drops. During an acute anxiety moment: 1 drop on the palm, rub hands together, cup over nose and mouth, breathe deeply 5 times.
Why it works: Linalool activates the parasympathetic nervous system via olfactory pathways, reducing sympathetic (fight-or-flight) activation within minutes. The fastest-acting natural anxiolytic available. Multiple clinical trials in pre-surgical anxiety, ICU anxiety, and generalised anxiety all confirm significant effect.

Oil 2 — Uplifting

Bergamot (Citrus bergamia)

How to use: 3–4 drops diffused. Do not apply to skin before sun exposure (photosensitive).
Why it works: Bergamot is the only citrus oil with both uplifting and calming properties simultaneously — it reduces anxiety without causing drowsiness. It's the flavour in Earl Grey tea, where it has been used for this purpose for centuries. Particularly useful for the anxiety-depression combination that is common during hormonal transitions.

Oil 3 — Deepening

Frankincense (Boswellia sacra or carteri)

How to use: 2 drops on the chest or diffused during meditation or slow breathing practice. 1 drop on the forehead (diluted) during acupressure practice.
Why it works: Incensole acetate in frankincense activates TRPV3 channels in the brain, producing a distinctly spiritual or expansive quality of calm — different from sedation. It slows the breathing rate, which directly reduces heart rate and anxiety. Used for thousands of years in meditation and ritual for exactly this reason. Pairs with the GV20 acupressure practice.

Daily anxiety protocol

  1. Morning: Ashwagandha (300mg) with breakfast + lemon balm tea
  2. Morning practice (10–15 min): PC6, HT7, LR3 — 2 minutes each side. Frankincense diffused or on chest.
  3. During the day: Passionflower tea or tincture, second lemon balm tea
  4. Moment of acute anxiety: Lavender on palm → breathe 5 slow breaths → press PC6 for 2 minutes
  5. Evening: Ashwagandha (300mg) with dinner. Bergamot diffused.
  6. Before bed: GV20 (light head massage) + HT7 + PC6

What works fastest vs what works deepest

  • Immediate (minutes): Lavender inhalation, PC6 acupressure, slow breathing
  • Short-term (1–2 weeks): Passionflower, lemon balm — daily use accumulates
  • Medium-term (4–8 weeks): Ashwagandha — cortisol regulation builds over time
  • Long-term: Regular acupressure practice reshapes the nervous system's baseline reactivity

The combination approach works better than any single intervention — you are supporting the nervous system from multiple angles simultaneously.
